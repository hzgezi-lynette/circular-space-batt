The Mission

Space sustainability has become a critical global concern. With active satellites projected to reach between 60,000 and 100,000 by 2030, the rapid expansion in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) creates unprecedented challenges regarding orbital congestion and debris mitigation.

Crucially, abandoned batteries are not just passive junk; they are active threats. Historically, 30% to 40% of accidental fragmentation events in orbit have been linked to energy system failures. Given that batteries account for 10% to 20% of a spacecraft’s dry mass, leaving them as "zombie" mass represents a massive loss of embedded energy and material investment.

Advancing Circularity & the ISAM Economy

Addressing these risks requires a paradigm shift from "disposable" hardware to sustainable, circular design. This workshop explores systems designed for reuse, repair, and material recovery. This shift aligns with the emerging In-space Servicing, Assembly, and Manufacturing (ISAM) market, projected to reach $14.3 billion by 2030. Enabling in-orbit battery replacement could extend the functional life of billion-dollar assets by 3 to 5 years.

A Cross-Disciplinary Dialogue

Solving the battery circularity challenge requires a complex interplay of:

  • ⚙️ Technical Pathways: Design-for-disassembly, robotic refueling/servicing interfaces, and modular energy storage.
  • ⚖️ Policy & Governance: New regulatory frameworks for orbital resource management and "Right to Repair" in space.
  • 📈 Economic Incentives: Funding models from national research councils, space agencies, and private sustainability programs.
